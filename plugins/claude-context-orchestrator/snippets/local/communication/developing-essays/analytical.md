# Analytical Essay Rules

Rules specific to academic papers, case studies, literary analysis, and analytical writing.

**Prerequisites**: Read `SKILL.md` universal rules first.

---

## Rule A1: Background vs Analysis Distinction

**Description**: Clearly separate established facts from your interpretation.

**Elaboration**: Background = established facts needed to understand. Analysis = your interpretation using those facts. Test each sentence: "Is this my argument or common knowledge?" If it's common knowledge, it's background. If it's your claim, it needs evidence.

**Example**:
- ❌ "The Snail Girl story served a purpose after the An Lushan rebellion." (Claim presented as fact)
- ✅ "The Snail Girl story reflects post-rebellion anxiety, as evidenced by [textual detail] and the increased presence of courtesan figures in [source]." (Claim + evidence)

---

## Rule A2: Evidence Triangle

**Description**: Every claim needs three types of support: textual, contextual, and logical.

**Elaboration**: Textual evidence: quote or detail from the source. Contextual support: historical or cultural information that makes the interpretation plausible. Logical connection: explicit explanation of how evidence supports claim. Missing any leg weakens the argument.

**Example**:
- ❌ "The protagonist's journey represents the immigrant experience." (claim only)
- ✅ "The protagonist's journey represents the immigrant experience: her repeated phrase 'I don't belong here' [textual] echoes the language of displacement common in 1920s diaspora literature [contextual], suggesting the author used her character to voice collective alienation [logical]."

---

## Rule A3: Precision Over Drama

**Description**: Academic credibility comes from understatement, not emphatic language.

**Elaboration**: Remove intensifiers that add rhetorical force without increasing precision: "any", "every", "definitive", "clear", "obvious". Let facts do the work. The subtle version is more damning because it's credible.

**Example**:
- ❌ "The regime's actions represent a definitive end to any hope of democracy."
- ✅ "The regime's actions ended democratic participation." (Let the reader feel the weight)

---

## Rule A4: No Melodrama

**Description**: Avoid dystopian or sensationalist phrasing that triggers skepticism.

**Elaboration**: Readers dismiss writing that feels like propaganda. The more damning a fact, the more flatly it should be stated. Melodrama invites dismissal; clinical precision invites belief.

**Example**:
- ❌ "Every name has been vetted for patriotism by national security police in a chilling display of authoritarianism."
- ✅ "No opposition candidates appear on the ballot; all have been screened by a vetting committee advised by national security police."

---

## Rule A5: Complete Phrases Over Fragments

**Description**: Use complete sentences rather than punchy fragments.

**Elaboration**: Fragment style ("Most striking:") feels journalistic and breathless. Complete phrases ("What is most striking is that...") feel authoritative. Reserve fragments for deliberate rhetorical effect, not as default.

**Example**:
- ❌ "Pro-democracy seats: zero. Independent voices: silenced."
- ✅ "The legislature now contains zero pro-democracy seats, and independent voices have been systematically excluded."

---

## Rule A6: Substantiate Empirical Claims

**Description**: Every factual assertion needs a source or qualification.

**Elaboration**: If you claim "emigrants faced hardship," find the source: report, statistic, interview. If you can't cite it, qualify it ("reportedly", "according to interviews") or remove it. Unsubstantiated empirical claims undermine the entire argument.

**Example**:
- ❌ "Emigrants have struggled to find work matching their qualifications."
- ✅ "According to a 2023 British Future report, trained accountants among Hong Kong emigrants are now working in kitchens."

---

## Rule A7: Context Before Events

**Description**: Establish context before narrating what happened.

**Elaboration**: The reader needs to understand 2019 before you discuss the 2020 National Security Law. Structural logic means related content stays proximate, and setup precedes action. Don't make readers backtrack.

**Example**:
- ❌ "The National Security Law was implemented in 2020. This followed the 2019 protests."
- ✅ "The 2019 protests—which brought millions to the streets—set the stage for Beijing's response. In 2020, the National Security Law was implemented."

---

## Rule A8: Explain Symbolic Dimensions

**Description**: When terms have significance beyond literal meaning, make it explicit.

**Elaboration**: Readers need to understand why actors care. "Hong Kong poses no physical threat to Beijing" might seem obvious—but then why the crackdown? Add: "The threat Hong Kong represents is symbolic and psychological." The symbolic layer isn't obvious; state it.

**Example**:
- ❌ "Beijing views Hong Kong's resistance as a threat."
- ✅ "Hong Kong poses no physical threat to Beijing—the city has no army, no nuclear weapons. The threat is symbolic: a successful example of Chinese people living under rule of law would undermine the Party's claim that authoritarianism is necessary for Chinese prosperity."

---

## Rule A9: No Staccato Sequences

**Description**: Combine series of short declarative sentences into flowing prose.

**Elaboration**: "Seventy percent held degrees. Sixty percent had children. Ninety-nine percent planned to stay." feels choppy. Combine into one sentence with natural rhythm. Reserve staccato for deliberate rhetorical effect.

**Example**:
- ❌ "Seventy percent held university degrees. Sixty percent had children under 18. Ninety-nine percent planned to stay permanently."
- ✅ "Seventy percent held university degrees, sixty percent had children under 18, and ninety-nine percent planned to stay permanently."

---

## Rule A10: Specific Conclusions

**Description**: Conclusions must name implications, not gesture at them.

**Elaboration**: "...and a warning that extends far beyond Hong Kong's borders" is lazy. What warning? To whom? Name it: "...and a warning to Taiwan, where 'one country, two systems' was once a viable proposal."

**Example**:
- ❌ "These events carry implications for the entire region."
- ✅ "These events demonstrate that Beijing will sacrifice economic interests—Hong Kong's value as a financial hub—to maintain political control, suggesting similar calculus may apply to Taiwan."

---

## Quick Checklist

- [ ] A1: Background and analysis clearly distinguished?
- [ ] A2: Every claim has textual + contextual + logical support?
- [ ] A3: Removed emphatic intensifiers ("any", "every", "clear")?
- [ ] A4: No melodramatic or sensationalist phrasing?
- [ ] A5: Complete sentences, not journalistic fragments?
- [ ] A6: Every empirical claim sourced or qualified?
- [ ] A7: Context established before events narrated?
- [ ] A8: Symbolic dimensions made explicit?
- [ ] A9: No choppy staccato sentence sequences?
- [ ] A10: Conclusion names specific implications?
