# Mentor Essay Rules

Rules specific to "person who influenced you" essays, thank-you notes, and relationship-focused prompts.

**Prerequisites**: Read `SKILL.md` universal rules first.

---

## Rule M1: Relationship Over Achievement

**Description**: Focus on what the person taught you, not what you accomplished.

**Elaboration**: These essays are about the relationship, not your resume. "I led 493 members" centers you. "You taught me that persistence matters more than perfection" centers the relationship and what you learned from it.

**Example**:
- ❌ "Under their mentorship, I led 493 members, organized weekly games, and created three new mentorship programs."
- ✅ "You taught me that access defines opportunity. When I saw hierarchy forming in our group, I remembered your words and introduced rotating moderators—your lesson applied in practice."

---

## Rule M2: Specific Lesson Attribution

**Description**: Name the specific lesson you learned from this person.

**Elaboration**: Generic influence ("they inspired me") is weak. What exactly did they teach you? Quote their words if possible. Show how a specific thing they said or did changed your thinking or behavior.

**Example**:
- ❌ "My coach inspired me to be a better person and work harder."
- ✅ "When you accepted me despite my 33% win rate, you taught me that potential matters more than current performance. I've applied this when recruiting new players—looking for enthusiasm over skill."

---

## Rule M3: Seven-Part Structure

**Description**: Follow this structure for comprehensive mentor essays.

**Elaboration**:
1. **Setting**: Where/how you met
2. **Relationship**: Who they are to you
3. **Transition**: How they empowered you
4. **Problems**: Challenges in the space
5. **Your actions**: What you did, influenced by them
6. **Growth**: What you learned from the process
7. **Thank you**: Connect back to their specific impact

**Example**:
- ❌ [Unstructured narrative jumping between memories]
- ✅ "We met at the informatics olympiad training camp [setting]. You became my first real mentor in competitive programming [relationship]. When you showed me how to analyze problems systematically [transition], I began to see past the immediate challenge [empowerment]..."

---

## Rule M4: Their Voice Present

**Description**: Include at least one direct quote or paraphrase of their words.

**Elaboration**: The mentor's voice should appear in the essay. What did they actually say? If you can't remember exact words, paraphrase: "You told me something like..." or "Your advice was essentially..." This makes the relationship concrete.

**Example**:
- ❌ "They gave me good advice about research."
- ✅ "You said: 'The fieldwork I'm proudest of came from conversations I almost didn't have.' I think of this every time I hesitate to approach someone."

---

## Rule M5: Observable Change

**Description**: Show observable behavior change, not just internal shift.

**Elaboration**: "I now think differently" is invisible. "I now do X when Y happens" is observable. The reader should be able to verify the change if they watched you. What would they see you doing differently?

**Example**:
- ❌ "After learning from them, I came to appreciate the value of patience."
- ✅ "After learning from you, I changed how I coach beginners. Instead of correcting immediately, I now ask 'What did you notice?' and let them discover the problem themselves."

---

## Rule M6: Observations as Relationship Lens

**Description**: Frame intellectual observations through the relationship.

**Elaboration**: You can include sociological or intellectual insights, but frame them as things the mentor helped you discover. Use observations to explain what the person taught you, not to showcase your own knowledge.

**Example**:
- ❌ "I observed that in-game economies create social hierarchies. I implemented rotating moderators to address this."
- ✅ "You taught me that access defines opportunity. When I saw the in-game economy creating hierarchy, I remembered your lesson and introduced rotating moderators."

---

## Rule M7: Gratitude Specificity

**Description**: Thank them for something specific, not general influence.

**Elaboration**: "Thank you for everything" is weak. "Thank you for telling me to talk to people more—that single piece of advice changed how I approach fieldwork" is specific. The thank-you should mirror the specific lesson.

**Example**:
- ❌ "I'm grateful for all you've done for me."
- ✅ "Thank you for accepting me when others wouldn't. That act of belief taught me to offer the same to others."

---

## Rule M8: No Hero Worship

**Description**: Present the mentor as human, not idealized.

**Elaboration**: Perfect mentors feel fictional. Acknowledge complexity: maybe they were demanding, or you disagreed at first, or their advice didn't make sense until later. Realistic portrayal is more credible than hagiography.

**Example**:
- ❌ "They were the perfect mentor who always knew exactly what to say."
- ✅ "At first, your advice frustrated me. 'Talk to people more' seemed too simple. It took a year before I understood what you meant—not more conversations, but different ones."

---

## Quick Checklist

- [ ] M1: Essay centers the relationship, not your achievements?
- [ ] M2: Specific lesson clearly attributed to this person?
- [ ] M3: All seven structural elements present?
- [ ] M4: At least one quote or paraphrase of their words?
- [ ] M5: Observable behavior change described?
- [ ] M6: Observations framed through relationship lens?
- [ ] M7: Thank-you is specific, not generic?
- [ ] M8: Mentor portrayed as human, not idealized?
