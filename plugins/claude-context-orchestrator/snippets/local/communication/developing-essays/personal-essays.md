# Personal Essays

Rules for essays about yourself: college applications, PhD statements, "Why X" essays, mentor essays, personal narratives.

**Core principle**: Personal essays answer "what will you do?" not "who are you?" Every theme must translate to future behavior.

---

## Core Rules

### P1: One Throughline

**Rule**: An essay has exactly one throughline. If you have multiple themes, subtract until one remains.

**Elaboration**: List all themes, write "This essay is about [theme]" for each, identify which feels most urgent, cut everything else. Multiple themes create unfocused essays that readers forget.

**Example**:
- MULTIPLE THEMES: Leadership, resilience, intellectual curiosity, community building
- TEST: "This essay is about leadership" / "This essay is about resilience" / etc.
- DECISION: "Resilience" feels most urgent → cut the others
- RESULT: Every paragraph now serves the resilience throughline

### P2: Actionability Test

**Rule**: Every theme must predict specific future behavior. If you can't name 3 concrete behaviors, the theme is too abstract.

**Elaboration**: Ask "What does this predict about future behavior?" Strong themes generate specific actions; weak themes generate vague intentions.

**Example**:
- WEAK: "I learned resilience" → (What specifically will you DO?)
- STRONG: "I embrace imperfection" →
  1. "I will take intellectual risks in seminars"
  2. "I will share preliminary research ideas before they're polished"
  3. "I will publish null results when experiments fail"

### P3: Forward Projection

**Rule**: Transform past experiences into future capabilities using the template: "Because [experience], I am now capable of [specific action]."

**Elaboration**: Past-focused essays describe; future-focused essays predict. Admissions wants to know what you'll do, not just what you did.

**Example**:
- PAST-FOCUSED: "I had to reinvent myself after injury"
- FUTURE-FOCUSED: "I reinvented myself once; I can do it again. When research hits dead ends, I will pivot rather than persist blindly."

### P4: Realization → Action

**Rule**: Every realization must include a behavior change. Use the pattern: "I realized [insight]. Now when [situation], I [specific behavior]."

**Elaboration**: Essays often end with insights but no behavior change. The insight alone is incomplete—show how it changed your actions.

**Example**:
- WEAK: "I realized food negotiates belonging"
- STRONG: "I realized food negotiates belonging. Now when roommates mention what they eat, I ask about the story behind it—not just the cuisine, but who taught them to cook it."

### P5: Circular Narrative

**Rule**: If opening establishes a theme, conclusion must show how that theme manifests in future action.

**Elaboration**: The opening and conclusion must connect. Test: Replace conclusion with opening theme phrase—does it connect naturally? If not, revise conclusion to explicitly callback.

**Example**:
- OPENING: "I'd grown through the words of others"
- WEAK CONCLUSION: "Bridge-building is carried in everyday objects" (insight, no callback)
- STRONG CONCLUSION: "At NYU, I'll grow others through my questions—not just learning from their words, but helping them discover meaning in their stories" (callbacks to "words of others" + shows future behavior)

### P6: Concrete Translation

**Rule**: Replace every abstraction with a concrete moment. If you can't name the moment, the concept isn't earned.

**Elaboration**: Abstract themes need grounding in specific experiences across multiple contexts.

**Example**:
- ABSTRACT: "I embrace imperfection"
- CONCRETE MOMENT: "In the lab, when I killed the cricket, I documented the failure and adjusted technique"
- THREE CONTEXTS:
  - Academic: "I share preliminary ideas in class before they're polished"
  - Research: "I publish null results when experiments fail"
  - Collaborative: "I admit when I don't know rather than bluffing"

### P7: Strong Opening Hook

**Rule**: Openings must establish stakes before delivering insights. Could the essay start at paragraph 2? If yes, paragraph 1 is weak.

**Elaboration**: Generic statements about generation, society, or abstract concepts feel like padding. Start with tension, surprise, or vivid scene.

**Example**:
- WEAK: "Growing up a member of Gen Z, I'm invested in learning how people negotiate power..."
- STRONG (tension): "The village preserved matriarchal tradition. Yet every woman I met had left to work in coastal cities."
- STRONG (surprise): "I expected to learn interviewing tactics. Instead, he told me to stop taking notes."
- STRONG (scene): "The boy approached singing. His mother, 2,000 miles away, had taught him the melody over FaceTime."

### P8: So What? Chain

**Rule**: Ask "So what?" three times to reach actionable insight. Stop at the third level.

**Elaboration**: Surface insights are rarely actionable. Dig deeper until you reach specific behaviors.

**Example**:
- LEVEL 1: "I embrace imperfection"
- SO WHAT? → "I'm willing to be vulnerable"
- SO WHAT? → "I take intellectual risks"
- SO WHAT? → "I contribute bold hypotheses in seminars, even if wrong"
- STOP: Third level is the actionable insight

### P9: One Sentence Test

**Rule**: The writer must complete: "This essay is about how [experience] taught me [insight], which means I will [action]."

**Elaboration**: If they can't complete this sentence, the essay isn't ready. All three parts—experience, insight, action—must be present.

**Example**:
- INCOMPLETE: "This essay is about how competitive programming taught me perseverance"
- COMPLETE: "This essay is about how losing IOI Bronze taught me that structural barriers require self-direction, which means I will build resources for others facing the same obstacles"

## PhD Research Statement Rules

Additional rules for PhD research statements. Apply Core Rules first.

### PhD1: Specific Research Interest by Paragraph 3

**Rule**: Reader must grasp your SPECIFIC research interest by the end of paragraph 3.

**Elaboration**: Vague interest ("I'm interested in algorithms") fails. Specific interest ("I want to understand what graph structures enable efficient algorithms, and where limits arise") succeeds.

**Example**:
- VAGUE: "I'm interested in theoretical computer science"
- SPECIFIC: "I want to design fast algorithms by understanding the combinatorial structures that make them possible—and prove where those structures reach their limits"

### PhD2: Opening Hook as Thread

**Rule**: Start with a compelling question, develop it throughout, circle back in conclusion. Don't abandon hook after paragraph 1.

**Elaboration**: The opening question should be the essay's spine. Every section should connect back to it.

**Example**:
- OPENING: "How can I compute the shortest path efficiently?"
- BODY: Each paragraph shows how this question evolved (IOI → Cambridge → research → faculty fit)
- CONCLUSION: "The question that started my journey—how to compute the shortest path—has become a question about the nature of efficient computation itself."

### PhD3: Capability Development, Not Event Listing

**Rule**: Each paragraph develops a CAPABILITY, not just lists an event. Show what you became able to do.

**Elaboration**: "I did X" is weak. "Doing X made me capable of Y" is strong.

**Example**:
- EVENT LISTING: "I attended lectures, won Bronze at ICPC, did an internship"
- CAPABILITY: "The Tripos gave me a systematic approach to proof. ICPC Bronze taught me to optimize under pressure. The internship showed me I prefer discovering algorithms to deploying them."

### PhD4: Transition Logic

**Rule**: Every paragraph must answer: "Why does this follow from the previous one?"

**Elaboration**: Map explicit transitions:
- IOI → University: What was LACKING in self-study?
- University → Internship: What did structured learning clarify?
- Internship → Research: How did industry confirm you want academia?
- Research → Next Project: How does this EXPAND your toolkit?

**Example**:
- WEAK: "I then did a summer internship at Hudson River Trading."
- STRONG: "Self-study built intuition, but I needed rigorous mathematical training. The Tripos provided this—and clarified that my interest was theoretical, not applied."

### PhD5: Authentic Research Reflection

**Rule**: Reflections must be specific to YOUR experience, not generic wisdom ("research requires perseverance").

**Elaboration**: What did YOU specifically learn that someone else might not have? What would you do differently?

**Example**:
- GENERIC: "Research taught me that perseverance is important"
- SPECIFIC: "I learned to survey literature first—we could have saved weeks knowing the original author was also stuck. I also learned that self-doubt during genuine uncertainty is normal, not a sign of inadequacy."

### PhD6: Faculty Fit with Intellectual Engagement

**Rule**: Connect SPECIFIC faculty work to YOUR specific interest. Show engagement with their IDEAS, not just name-dropping.

**Elaboration**: Don't say "aligns with my interests." Show how their work connects to your questions.

**Example**:
- WEAK: "Professor Williams' research aligns with my interests in algorithms"
- STRONG: "Professor Williams' work on shortest-path algorithms—the very question that first captivated me at that IOI workshop—has developed into research on graph pattern detection that directly informs my interest in understanding exploitable graph structures"

### PhD7: No Weakness Admissions

**Rule**: Never admit weakness ("Although I lack training in..."). Reframe as growth opportunity.

**Elaboration**: Weakness admissions invite doubt. Show self-directed interest instead.

**Example**:
- WEAK: "Although I lack formal training in parallel computing..."
- STRONG: "My interest in distributed algorithms has been self-directed; Professor Ghaffari's work offers structured depth in this frontier"

### PhD8: Center Target School, Not Current Institution

**Rule**: Don't praise your current institution. Center the target school.

**Elaboration**: "Cambridge taught me that..." centers Cambridge. "Through my research, I've learned that..." centers your growth and the target school.

**Example**:
- WEAK: "My years at Cambridge have shown me the value of intellectual community"
- STRONG: "MIT's theory community offers the concentration of researchers and ideas I need to develop as a scholar"

### PhD9: Post-PhD Goals

**Rule**: Mention what you'll do after the PhD. Forward projection extends beyond graduation.

**Elaboration**: Show you've thought beyond the program itself.

**Example**:
- MISSING: [Essay ends with "I look forward to joining MIT"]
- PRESENT: "After my PhD, I aim to continue closing gaps between upper and lower bounds, developing faster algorithms, and making fundamental discoveries about the limits of efficient computation."

## PhD Personal Statement Rules

For the "personal" essay when application has two essays. Apply Core Rules first.

### PPS1: Different Angle from Research Statement

**Rule**: If both essays mention the same experience, use different angles.

**Elaboration**: Research statement → intellectual curiosity angle. Personal statement → perseverance/community angle.

**Example**:
- EXPERIENCE: IOI journey
- RESEARCH STATEMENT ANGLE: "The question of computing shortest paths efficiently captivated me"
- PERSONAL STATEMENT ANGLE: "Switzerland lacked coaches who could guide students beyond Bronze. Rather than accepting this, I taught myself—and now ensure future students won't face the same barriers"

### PPS2: Meaning Beyond Self

**Rule**: Show that what you do has meaning beyond your own achievement.

**Elaboration**: Good throughlines: "What I do should have meaning beyond myself" / "I thrive when lowering barriers for others"

**Example**:
- SELF-FOCUSED: "I won Gold at IOI through determination"
- BEYOND SELF: "Winning Gold mattered less than what came after: I became what I lacked. Since 2021, I've volunteered as coach and mentor for the Swiss IOI team"

## "Why This College/Program" Rules

Apply Core Rules first.

### WTC1: Specificity Over Name-Dropping

**Rule**: Show what you'd actually DO, not just list programs. Test: Could another applicant copy-paste this sentence? If yes, add specificity.

**Elaboration**: Generic references to programs fail. Specific projects/questions succeed.

**Example**:
- GENERIC: "I'm excited to join debates at the Philomathean Society"
- SPECIFIC: "At Philomathean Society, I want to bring debates on digital policy—how do we regulate platforms that shape identity formation?"

### WTC2: Program → Question → Values

**Rule**: Every program mention follows: Program/opportunity → specific question/project you'd pursue → why this connects to your values.

**Elaboration**: Don't just say you'll use a resource. Say what question you'll investigate with it.

**Example**:
- WEAK: "I'll use the Data Driven Discovery Initiative"
- STRONG: "Through DDDI, I plan to analyze social media discourse patterns using NLP to understand how marginalized communities build counter-narratives—extending my undergraduate work on digital belonging"

## Mentor/Thank-You Essay Rules

Apply Core Rules first.

### MTE1: Relationship Focus Over Achievement Focus

**Rule**: Frame achievements through what the mentor taught you, not as standalone accomplishments.

**Elaboration**: The essay is about the relationship and what you learned from them, not a resume of what you did.

**Example**:
- ACHIEVEMENT-FOCUSED: "I led 493 members, organized games, created mentorship programs..."
- RELATIONSHIP-FOCUSED: "You taught me that persistence matters more than perfection. When you accepted me despite my 33% win rate, I learned that potential matters more than track record."

### MTE2: Seven-Part Structure

**Rule**: Follow this structure for mentor essays:
1. Introduce setting (where/how you met)
2. Establish mentor relationship (who they are to you)
3. Show transition (how they empowered you)
4. Present problems (challenges in the space)
5. Detail your actions (influenced by them)
6. Reflect on growth (what you learned)
7. Final thank you (connect back to their specific impact)

**Elaboration**: This structure ensures you cover the relationship arc, not just list accomplishments.

**Example**:
- WEAK: Jumping straight to "I implemented rotating moderators..."
- STRONG: "You taught me that access defines opportunity. When I saw the PeiWan economy create hierarchy in our group, I remembered your words and introduced rotating moderators..."

## Self-Check Checklist

Before submitting any personal essay:

- [ ] Can I state the ONE throughline in one sentence?
- [ ] Does throughline predict 3+ specific future behaviors?
- [ ] Does conclusion callback to opening theme?
- [ ] Are all reflections specific (not "perseverance")?
- [ ] Is every abstraction grounded in a concrete moment?
- [ ] Does opening establish stakes (not generic statements)?
- [ ] Are all factual claims verified?
- [ ] Are all quotes checked against source?
- [ ] No weakness admissions?
- [ ] Would I not cringe reading this aloud?

**For PhD essays, also check:**
- [ ] Specific research interest clear by paragraph 3?
- [ ] Each paragraph develops capability, not just lists event?
- [ ] Faculty connections show intellectual engagement?
- [ ] Post-PhD goals mentioned?
- [ ] Target school centered (not current institution)?
