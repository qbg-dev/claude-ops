# PhD Research Statement Rules

Rules specific to PhD research statements, SOPs, and "describe your research interests" essays.

**Prerequisites**: Read `SKILL.md` universal rules first.

---

## Rule P1: Specific Interest by Paragraph 3

**Description**: Reader must grasp your specific research interest within the first three paragraphs.

**Elaboration**: Don't make readers wait until the middle to understand what you want to study. By the end of paragraph 3, a faculty member should be able to summarize your research interest in one sentence. Vague interests ("I'm interested in algorithms") fail this test.

**Example**:
- ❌ Paragraphs 1-3 discuss: IOI journey, Cambridge experience, internship → reader still doesn't know specific interest
- ✅ Paragraph 2: "I want to understand what graph structures enable efficient algorithms, and where the limits of efficiency lie"

---

## Rule P2: Opening Hook as Thread

**Description**: Start with a compelling question and develop it as a thread through the entire essay.

**Elaboration**: The opening hook should not be abandoned after paragraph 1. It should reappear—explicitly or implicitly—in the research description, faculty fit section, and conclusion. The hook transforms from question to journey to destination.

**Example**:
- ❌ Opens with "How can I compute the shortest path?" → never mentioned again after paragraph 1
- ✅ Opens with shortest path question → mentions it when describing research → connects to Professor Williams' work on shortest paths → conclusion circles back to "the question that started my journey"

---

## Rule P3: Combine Journey and Realization

**Description**: Merge "what happened" and "what I learned" into one narrative arc.

**Elaboration**: Don't separate competition journey from realization about deeper interests into different paragraphs. Weave the realization INTO the narrative as it unfolds. "I won Gold, and then I realized I wanted to understand why" becomes "The path to Gold revealed something deeper: I cared less about winning than about understanding why certain structures make solutions possible."

**Example**:
- ❌ Paragraph 1: "I competed in IOI and won Gold." Paragraph 2: "After winning, I realized I wanted to do research."
- ✅ "The path from Bronze to Gold took two years of self-study—but the destination mattered less than what I discovered along the way: my real interest was not in winning, but in understanding why certain structures make efficient computation possible."

---

## Rule P4: Transition Logic

**Description**: Every paragraph must answer "Why does this follow?"

**Elaboration**: The reader should never wonder why you're telling them something. Each transition should be motivated: IOI → University (what was lacking in self-study?), University → Internship (what did structured learning clarify?), Internship → Research (how did industry confirm you want academia?), Research → Next Project (how does this expand your toolkit?).

**Example**:
- ❌ "After Cambridge, I did an internship at HRT. The following summer, I did research with Prof. Dawar."
- ✅ "The internship clarified my direction: while industry applies algorithms, I want to discover why they work. This led me to seek research experience with Prof. Dawar."

---

## Rule P5: No Achievement Lists

**Description**: Don't list accomplishments without showing what they developed.

**Elaboration**: "I attended lectures, won Bronze, did an internship" is a resume, not a narrative. Each experience should demonstrate a capability you developed. Transform lists into development arcs.

**Example**:
- ❌ "I regularly attended algorithm lectures, represented Cambridge at ICPC World Finals, did an internship at HRT, and undertook research with Prof. Dawar."
- ✅ "The Tripos gave me systematic proof techniques that proved essential in research; ICPC World Finals showed me the gap between solving problems and understanding why solutions work."

---

## Rule P6: Faculty Fit Specificity

**Description**: Connect specific faculty work to your specific interest.

**Elaboration**: Don't just name-drop professors with "aligns with my interests." Show intellectual engagement with their actual research. Cite specific papers or results. If your opening hook connects to faculty work, make it explicit.

**Example**:
- ❌ "Professor Williams' research aligns well with my interests in graph algorithms."
- ✅ "Professor Williams' work on shortest path algorithms—the very question that first captivated me at that workshop—represents exactly the kind of fundamental inquiry I want to pursue. Her recent results on graph pattern detection connect to my interest in understanding what structures make efficient search possible."

---

## Rule P7: No "I Hope To"

**Description**: Replace tentative language with confident intention.

**Elaboration**: "I hope to" signals uncertainty. Use "I aim to", "I intend to", "I will pursue." You're not hoping for permission—you're stating what you will do if admitted.

**Example**:
- ❌ "I hope to contribute to the field of theoretical computer science."
- ✅ "I aim to close gaps between upper and lower bounds for graph algorithms."

---

## Rule P8: Cut Resume Redundancy

**Description**: Don't repeat information that appears in your CV.

**Elaboration**: "Which I took the summer after my second year" adds nothing—the resume has dates. Focus on what the experience taught you or developed, not when it happened.

**Example**:
- ❌ "The internship at Hudson River Trading, which I undertook in the summer following my second year of the Mathematical Tripos..."
- ✅ "The HRT internship gave me a quantitative approach to optimization that I hadn't encountered in pure mathematics."

---

## Rule P9: Side Projects as Toolkit Expansion

**Description**: Frame non-central work as expanding your methodological toolkit.

**Elaboration**: Don't say "I enjoy areas outside my main focus"—this suggests scattered attention. Instead, frame side projects as deliberate expansion. "Seeking to expand my methodological toolkit, I joined..."

**Example**:
- ❌ "I've always enjoyed exploring different areas of mathematics outside my main focus."
- ✅ "Working with Prof. Gowers on automated theorem proving introduced me to techniques quite different from algorithm design—methodologies I expect will inform how I approach proofs in my primary research."

---

## Rule P10: Center Target School

**Description**: Make the target program the focus, not your current institution.

**Elaboration**: "Cambridge taught me that..." centers Cambridge. "Through my research, I've learned that..." centers your development. The essay should make the reader think about you at their institution, not at your current one.

**Example**:
- ❌ "My years at Cambridge have taught me that intellectual growth is best achieved through shared curiosity."
- ✅ "MIT's theory community offers the concentration of researchers and ideas I need to develop as a scholar."

---

## Rule P11: Post-PhD Goals

**Description**: Mention what you intend to do after the PhD.

**Elaboration**: Admissions committees want to see you've thought beyond the degree. Even one sentence about research career goals shows you understand the PhD as a step, not an endpoint.

**Example**:
- ❌ Essay ends with: "I look forward to joining MIT's theory group."
- ✅ Essay ends with: "After my PhD, I aim to continue closing gaps between upper and lower bounds—contributing new techniques for understanding the fundamental limits of efficient computation."

---

## Rule P12: Authentic Research Reflection

**Description**: Describe what research actually felt like, not what you think admissions wants to hear.

**Elaboration**: Generic reflections ("perseverance is important") signal you haven't done real research. Authentic reflections describe specific challenges: "When you're stuck, you can't tell if failure comes from the problem's difficulty or your own limitations."

**Example**:
- ❌ "This experience taught me that research requires perseverance and determination."
- ✅ "For much of the project, I was stuck—and in a realm of genuine uncertainty, you can't distinguish whether failure comes from the problem's difficulty or your own limitations. What helped was discovering that the original author had also made no progress: the problem was genuinely hard, not just hard for me."

---

## Quick Checklist

- [ ] P1: Specific research interest clear by paragraph 3?
- [ ] P2: Opening hook threaded throughout and revisited?
- [ ] P3: Journey and realization woven together?
- [ ] P4: Every transition motivated?
- [ ] P5: Experiences show development, not just list events?
- [ ] P6: Faculty connections cite specific work?
- [ ] P7: No "I hope to" language?
- [ ] P8: No CV-redundant dates/details?
- [ ] P9: Side projects framed as toolkit expansion?
- [ ] P10: Target school centered, not current institution?
- [ ] P11: Post-PhD goals mentioned?
- [ ] P12: Research reflection authentic and specific?
