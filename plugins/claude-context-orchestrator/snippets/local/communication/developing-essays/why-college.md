# "Why This School" Essay Rules

Rules specific to school-specific supplements, "Why X University" essays, and program fit statements.

**Prerequisites**: Read `SKILL.md` universal rules first.

---

## Rule W1: Specificity Over Name-Dropping

**Description**: Show what you'd actually do, not just list programs you've heard of.

**Elaboration**: Naming a program ("the Data Driven Discovery Initiative") proves you read the website. Describing what you'd do with it proves you've thought about fit. Every program mention should include a specific project, question, or activity you'd pursue.

**Example**:
- ❌ "I'm excited to take advantage of the Data Driven Discovery Initiative."
- ✅ "Through DDDI, I plan to analyze social media discourse patterns using NLP to understand how marginalized communities build counter-narratives."

---

## Rule W2: Copy-Paste Test

**Description**: Could another applicant use this sentence unchanged?

**Elaboration**: If the answer is yes, the sentence is too generic. Every sentence should contain something specific to you AND specific to the school. "I'm excited to join debates at the Philomathean Society" passes for the school but fails for you—any applicant could write it.

**Example**:
- ❌ "I'm excited to join debates at the Philomathean Society." (any applicant could write this)
- ✅ "At Philomathean Society, I want to bring debates on digital policy—how do we regulate platforms that shape identity formation?" (specific to your interests + the program)

---

## Rule W3: Values → Opportunities Connection

**Description**: Connect your values to specific opportunities, not vice versa.

**Elaboration**: Lead with what you care about, then show how the school's offerings serve that. Don't start with "X University has..." Start with "I want to understand X. At [University], Professor Y's work on Z and the Q program offer exactly the environment for this."

**Example**:
- ❌ "MIT has a strong theory of computation group with many excellent faculty."
- ✅ "I want to understand the limits of efficient computation. MIT's theory group—especially Professor Williams' work on shortest path algorithms and Professor Ghaffari's distributed computing research—represents the concentration of expertise I need."

---

## Rule W4: Avoid Generic Praise

**Description**: Cut sentences that praise the school without connecting to you.

**Elaboration**: "X University is renowned for its innovative approach to Y" is filler. The admissions committee knows their school is good. Use that word count to explain what YOU would do there.

**Example**:
- ❌ "NYU's Stern School of Business is world-renowned for its approach to combining business education with liberal arts."
- ✅ [Delete entirely—use the space for your specific plans]

---

## Rule W5: Research Faculty Before Writing

**Description**: Verify that professors actually work on what you claim.

**Elaboration**: Faculty research areas change. Before writing "Professor X works on Y," check their recent publications and current research page. Claiming interest in work they no longer do signals you didn't do your homework.

**Example**:
- ❌ "I'm drawn to Professor Smith's work on machine learning for healthcare." (She moved to industry two years ago)
- ✅ Check the department page, verify current faculty, cite a recent paper or ongoing project

---

## Rule W6: Beyond Academics

**Description**: Include at least one non-academic opportunity.

**Elaboration**: "Why This School" essays that only mention classes and research feel incomplete. Include one community, club, or cultural element that genuinely interests you. This shows you've thought about life there, not just coursework.

**Example**:
- ❌ [Essay mentions only courses and faculty]
- ✅ "Beyond the theory group, I'm drawn to MIT's handball club—I captained Cambridge's team and want to continue playing at a competitive level."

---

## Rule W7: No Location Clichés

**Description**: Don't mention location unless it's genuinely relevant to your plans.

**Elaboration**: "I'm excited about New York's vibrant cultural scene" could apply to thousands of applicants and dozens of schools. If location matters—because you need access to specific archives, communities, or industries—explain how. Otherwise, cut it.

**Example**:
- ❌ "Boston's rich history and intellectual atmosphere will inspire my studies."
- ✅ "Access to the MIT-Harvard library system is essential for my research on [specific topic]—the combined archives contain [specific resource]."

---

## Rule W8: Future Tense Commitment

**Description**: Write in future tense to show commitment, not conditional.

**Elaboration**: "I would take" and "I might explore" signal uncertainty. "I will pursue" and "I intend to" signal commitment. Admissions committees want students who have decided, not students still deliberating.

**Example**:
- ❌ "If admitted, I would consider exploring the intersection of..."
- ✅ "At [School], I will pursue research at the intersection of..."

---

## Quick Checklist

- [ ] W1: Every program mention includes your specific plan for it?
- [ ] W2: No sentence another applicant could copy unchanged?
- [ ] W3: Your values lead, school opportunities follow?
- [ ] W4: No generic praise of the school?
- [ ] W5: Faculty research verified as current?
- [ ] W6: At least one non-academic element included?
- [ ] W7: Location mentioned only if genuinely relevant?
- [ ] W8: Future tense commitment, not conditional language?
